# Photosynthesis Fixture Note

Photosynthesis is the process used by plants, algae, and some bacteria to convert light energy into chemical energy. In oxygenic photosynthesis, light energy is captured by pigments such as chlorophyll and used to power reactions that convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen. The overall idea is that light energy becomes stored chemical energy in sugar molecules.

Photosynthesis has two major stages. The light-dependent reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts. During these reactions, chlorophyll absorbs light, water molecules are split, oxygen is released, and energy-carrying molecules such as ATP and NADPH are produced. These energy carriers do not store food by themselves; they provide usable energy for the next stage.

The Calvin cycle, also called the light-independent reactions, occurs in the stroma of the chloroplast. It uses carbon dioxide along with ATP and NADPH from the light-dependent reactions to build sugar molecules. The Calvin cycle depends on products from the light-dependent reactions even though it does not directly require light.

Chloroplasts are organelles found in plant and algal cells. They contain chlorophyll, thylakoids, and stroma. Chlorophyll is important because it absorbs light energy. Thylakoids are membrane structures where the light-dependent reactions happen. The stroma is the fluid-filled space where the Calvin cycle takes place.

The inputs of photosynthesis are light energy, water, and carbon dioxide. The outputs are glucose and oxygen. Water is split during the light-dependent reactions, and oxygen is released as a byproduct. Carbon dioxide is used during the Calvin cycle to help build sugar.

Photosynthesis matters because it supplies chemical energy for many food webs and releases oxygen into the atmosphere. Plants use the glucose they produce for growth, storage, and cellular respiration. Animals and many other organisms depend directly or indirectly on photosynthesis because they consume plants or organisms that consumed plants.

Several factors can affect the rate of photosynthesis. More light can increase the rate until the plant reaches a limit. Carbon dioxide concentration can also limit the process. Temperature matters because the enzymes involved in photosynthesis work best within a certain range. Too little water can reduce photosynthesis because stomata may close to prevent water loss, which also limits carbon dioxide intake.

Common misconceptions include thinking that plants get most of their food from soil, thinking that oxygen is an input rather than an output, or thinking that the Calvin cycle is completely independent of the light-dependent reactions. Soil provides minerals and water, but the carbon in glucose mostly comes from carbon dioxide in the air.
